# General Referee Report — Method & Theory
Date: 2026-03-12
Mode: method

## What Works

The paper's architecture is genuinely clever: a single primitive (rho) threads through three established frameworks (Goldstein-Pauzner, Grossman-Stiglitz, Avellaneda-Stoikov), and the amplification loop connecting them is well-defined as a fixed-point problem with a clean Jacobian analysis. The information collapse lemma (Lemma 1) is correct and does real work throughout the paper. The bifurcation argument via the divergence of h near rho_1* is the strongest piece of mathematical reasoning in the manuscript and delivers the paper's central inequality rho* < min(rho_i*) convincingly.

## Summary

The theoretical apparatus is largely sound in structure but contains several points where assumptions do invisible work, where derivations skip steps that are not straightforward, and where the connection between the mathematical formalism and the economic mechanism is asserted rather than demonstrated. The most serious issues concern: (1) Assumption A1, which transfers a uniqueness condition from linear-quadratic games to the binary-action Goldstein-Pauzner setting without a proof that this transfer is valid; (2) Assumption A2, which posits a linear aggregation form for the Channel 3-to-Channel 1 link that is economically under-motivated; and (3) the treatment of the boundary rho_eff = rho_1* in the Brouwer argument, where continuity of the theta* function requires an equilibrium selection convention that is adopted without discussion of its consequences for the bifurcation result.

## Mode-Specific Assessment

### Assumption Plausibility
The key assumptions are: (A1) the uniqueness boundary for the binary-action GP game equals 1/(1+alpha_SC^2); (A2) the aggregation form g_1 is linear. A1 is non-standard and unproven for the binary-action case. A2 is a tractability assumption with under-specified microfoundation. The normalisation sigma_eta^2 = sigma_xi^2 = sigma^2 is clean and well-motivated.

### Information Structure Audit
Each agent observes x_i and conditions on it. Agents cannot condition on eta directly. The amplification loop assumes Channel 3's price dislocations feed into Channel 1's signal structure through g_1, but the mechanism by which depositors in the GP game observe or respond to market-making liquidity withdrawal is not explicit. This is a change in the information structure that needs clarification: are depositors observing market prices? If not, g_1 is a reduced-form correlation effect, weakening the microfoundation.

### Derivation Correctness
Lemma 1 (Fisher information): correct. N_eff formula: correct. Jacobian: algebraically correct. Saddle-node argument: correctly structured but non-degeneracy conditions not verified. Welfare formula: transfers results from continuous to binary actions without justification. RPE formula: assumes linear equilibrium decomposition.

### Results vs. Assumptions
The bifurcation result rho* < min(rho_i*) is a genuine consequence of the cross-channel feedback, not hard-wired by functional forms. The specific formula for rho_1* depends on A1. The amplification strength depends on the linear form of g_1 (A2). The N_eff formula is robust.

### Over-engineering
No over-engineering detected. Mathematical tools match the problem's complexity.

### Comparative Statics
All signs stated and economically interpreted. Gap: comparative statics of rho* in model parameters are in the working notes but not in the paper.

### Equilibrium Characterisation
Existence via Brouwer. Local uniqueness via spectral radius. Global uniqueness not established. Boundary treatment adequate but should be noted as a modelling choice.

### Fixed-Point Structure
Well-defined and correctly analysed. Basin of attraction not characterised, limiting calibration.

### Notation Consistency
Consistent throughout. Minor: tau and tau_s used interchangeably.

## Recommendation

MAJOR REVISION

The theoretical framework is well-constructed and the central result (rho* < min rho_i*) is genuinely interesting. The paper makes a clear contribution that no existing work delivers. However, two blocking issues must be resolved: Assumption A1 (the uniqueness boundary transfer from linear-quadratic to binary-action games) and Assumption A2 (the microfoundation for the Channel 3-to-Channel 1 aggregation). The saddle-node non-degeneracy conditions must be verified explicitly. The welfare analysis must be adapted to the binary-action setting. These are fixable problems, and the core architecture of the paper is sound.
