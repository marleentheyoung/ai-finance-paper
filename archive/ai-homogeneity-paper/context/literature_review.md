# Literature Review

## Introduction to the literature landscape

The rapid adoption of artificial intelligence in financial markets raises fundamental questions about how homogeneity in information processing affects systemic stability. This paper draws on three well-established theoretical traditions in financial economics: the global games literature on coordination failure, the Grossman-Stiglitz tradition on information acquisition and price efficiency, and the market microstructure literature on liquidity provision and withdrawal. Each of these literatures has developed sophisticated models of how information structures shape equilibrium outcomes, yet each has evolved largely in isolation from the others. The intersection of all three, connected through a common parameterisation of signal homogeneity, remains unexplored.

The contribution of this paper is to formalise how AI model homogeneity, captured by the cross-sectional correlation of AI-generated signals, simultaneously activates fragility mechanisms across all three domains and to characterise their mutual amplification. The existing literature has addressed AI and financial markets primarily through two lenses: reinforcement learning and behavioural convergence on one hand, and qualitative policy analysis on the other. A formal, information-theoretic treatment of how signal correlation propagates through coordination, information acquisition, and liquidity provision channels has not been attempted. This review maps the relevant literatures, identifies the precise mechanisms that have and have not been formalised, and establishes the gap that the amplification loop fills.

The review proceeds as follows. Sections on coordination games, information acquisition, market microstructure, and AI in finance survey each channel's foundations and identify the specific modelling gap. A section on cross-channel interaction establishes that no existing paper characterises the joint dynamics. A final section summarises the five contributions relative to the closest threat papers.

## Coordination games and common knowledge

The global games framework, introduced by Carlsson and van Damme (1993) and developed extensively by Morris and Shin (1998, 2002), resolved a longstanding problem in coordination game theory: the multiplicity of equilibria in games with strategic complementarities. By introducing small amounts of private noise around a common fundamental, Morris and Shin demonstrated that a unique threshold equilibrium obtains, restoring sharp comparative statics and eliminating sunspot-driven crises. Goldstein and Pauzner (2005) applied this framework to bank runs in the Diamond-Dybvig setting, deriving a unique crisis threshold as a function of demand deposit contract terms and establishing that the probability of bank runs can be computed as a function of model primitives.

The uniqueness result, however, depends critically on the information structure. Hellwig (2002) proved that sufficiently precise public information reinstates multiple equilibria, because agents can coordinate on the public signal rather than relying on their heterogeneous private signals. The Hellwig result establishes a boundary: when the precision of the public signal relative to private signals exceeds a threshold, the global game admits multiple equilibria and the uniqueness selection breaks down. Angeletos, Hellwig, and Pavan (2006) extended this insight by showing that endogenous policy signals can similarly restore multiplicity, and Angeletos and Pavan (2007) characterised the welfare implications of public information in coordination games, establishing conditions under which more precise public information is socially harmful.

The connection between AI model homogeneity and this literature is direct but has not been formalised. When financial agents rely on the same AI model to process information, the cross-sectional correlation of their private signals rises, progressively converting heterogeneous private information into a near-public common signal. This activates the Hellwig (2002) mechanism: as signal correlation increases, the effective precision of the common component grows relative to the idiosyncratic component, moving the economy toward the multiplicity region.

Two recent papers have studied AI in global games settings but through different mechanisms. Yang (2024) places Q-learning agents in a Morris-Shin speculative attack game and shows that reinforcement learning agents coordinate more effectively on crisis equilibria than human subjects or Nash equilibrium predictions. The mechanism is entirely behavioural: convergence occurs through repeated interaction and Q-value updating, not through the signal structure. There is no signal correlation parameter, and the crisis threshold is learned computationally rather than derived analytically. Danielsson and Uthemann (2025, JFS) develop a formal global games model where AI agents have a speed advantage in executing runs. Their crisis threshold, theta*(mu) = (c/b)[(1-mu)p + mu], is a function of the AI agent fraction mu and the human execution probability p. This captures the extensive margin of AI penetration but not the intensive margin of AI output homogeneity. Crucially, Danielsson and Uthemann discuss the Hellwig (2002) multiplicity result qualitatively in their Section 2.2.2 but do not integrate it into their formal model, which operates under maintained Morris-Shin uniqueness conditions.

The gap that this paper fills in Channel 1 is the analytical characterisation of the crisis threshold as a function of signal correlation (rho) in the Goldstein-Pauzner bank-run framework. The primitive is rho (AI signal correlation, intensive margin), not mu (AI agent fraction, extensive margin) or Q-value convergence (RL behavioural mechanism). The key result is that theta*(rho) is non-monotone, and the uniqueness/multiplicity boundary rho* is characterised as a function of signal correlation and strategic complementarity. High mu with diverse AI models (low rho) does not trigger this mechanism; low mu with a single dominant model (high rho among AI users) does. Szkup and Trevino (2015) endogenised information acquisition in global games and provided sufficient conditions for uniqueness; this paper assesses whether those conditions are violated when signals become correlated through a common AI source, providing an additional channel through which AI model homogeneity undermines equilibrium selection. Hansen and Lee (2025) find that independently queried AI agents reduce herding, which is consistent with the low-rho regime; the fragility mechanism of this paper activates at high rho, a regime their experiment does not test.

## Information acquisition and price efficiency

Grossman and Stiglitz (1980) established that informationally efficient markets are impossible when information is costly: if prices fully reflect private information, no agent has an incentive to pay the cost of acquiring it, but without information acquisition, prices cannot be informative. The resolution requires noise and partial revelation. Holden and Subrahmanyam (1992) showed that when multiple traders possess the same private information, competition among them leads to aggressive trading and rapid revelation, driving rents to zero. Goldstein and Yang (2015) demonstrated that information diversity creates strategic complementarities in information acquisition: when multiple dimensions of information exist, knowledge of one dimension increases the incentive to acquire others. Bond, Edmans, and Goldstein (2012) distinguished forecasting price efficiency (FPE), which measures how well prices predict future outcomes, from revelatory price efficiency (RPE), which measures whether prices reveal information not otherwise available to real decision-makers. The FPE/RPE distinction is central to evaluating the welfare consequences of AI in financial markets.

Several papers have studied how technology affects information acquisition. Dugast and Foucault (2018, JFE) model how data abundance reduces price informativeness through a speed-precision tradeoff: cheap, fast but imprecise signals crowd out demand for costly, slow but precise signals. The mechanism operates in the temporal dimension, modelling the choice between fast and slow signals for a single data source over time. Dugast and Foucault (2025, JF) extend this programme to equilibrium data mining, where computing power affects the threshold signal-to-noise ratio for predictor inclusion. Farboodi and Veldkamp (2020, AER) model the long-run growth of data processing technology and show that improvement can shift information production toward processing already-available public data rather than producing genuinely private signals. Farboodi, Matray, Veldkamp, and Venkateswaran (2022, RFS) document empirically that data technology concentrates information on large firms, leaving smaller firms informationally impoverished. Banerjee, Davis, and Gondhi (2018, RFS) show that greater transparency can paradoxically reduce informativeness by shifting learning from fundamentals to beliefs about others' information.

Vives (2014, JEEA) established that a privately revealing equilibrium obtains in competitive markets when correlation in traders' valuations is not too large. This result is directly relevant: it identifies the correlation threshold above which the Grossman-Stiglitz resolution fails. As AI drives cross-sectional signal correlation toward one, Vives's conditions for informational efficiency are progressively violated.

The gap that this paper fills in Channel 2 is a Grossman-Stiglitz extension with two information types differentiated by their cross-sectional correlation structure: AI-processed signals at near-zero cost with pairwise correlation rho, and genuinely private fundamental research at cost c_P with correlation near zero. The existing literature models temporal tradeoffs (speed vs. precision), growth of data technology, and concentration of information across assets. No paper models the cross-sectional dimension where N agents processing the same AI output produce signals with pairwise correlation rho, collapsing the effective information content even though each individual signal is precise. The key result combines the Holden-Subrahmanyam competition effect (correlated information yields zero rents, reducing incentives to acquire AI signals) with the Goldstein-Yang complementarity breakdown (homogenisation of one information dimension destroys complementarities in acquiring other dimensions), producing a non-monotone relationship between AI adoption (rho) and revelatory price efficiency. At low rho, AI improves FPE by making better predictions; at high rho, information diversity collapses and RPE degrades, harming real investment efficiency.

## Market microstructure and correlated liquidity

The foundational models of market microstructure, including Glosten and Milgrom (1985) and Kyle (1985), established that market makers widen spreads in response to adverse selection and that prices adjust to order flow information. Avellaneda and Stoikov (2008) provided the stochastic control framework for optimal market making in limit order books, yielding the reservation price formula that determines how market makers adjust quotes based on inventory, volatility, and time horizon. When multiple market makers calibrate on shared data using the same AI model, the Avellaneda-Stoikov framework implies that they produce near-identical spread-widening functions.

Several papers have studied related mechanisms of correlated liquidity behaviour. Greenwood and Thesmar (2011, JFE) defined stock fragility as the covariance of holders' demand shocks and showed that high fragility predicts future volatility and non-fundamental price movements. Their measure captures demand-side correlation from mutual fund flows. Danielsson, Shin, and Zigrand (2012, NBER) formalised how common VaR constraints produce endogenous risk: when multiple institutions use the same risk management framework, they respond identically to volatility shocks, generating procyclical selling that amplifies price impact. Brunnermeier and Pedersen (2009, RFS) modelled the feedback between market liquidity and funding liquidity, establishing that margin spirals can drain liquidity precisely when it is most needed.

On the AI side, Colliard, Foucault, and Lovo (2025, RFS) model a Q-learning algorithmic market maker in a Glosten-Milgrom framework and show that RL-based pricing produces non-competitive markups detectable through trading data. Their focus is the learning dynamics of a single algorithmic market maker, not the cross-sectional correlation among multiple market makers. Cespa and Vives (forthcoming, AER) show that market opacity creates strategic complementarity in liquidity demand, yielding multiple equilibria where liquidity evaporates suddenly. Their mechanism operates through information opacity, not through algorithmic similarity.

The gap that this paper fills in Channel 3 is the derivation of an effective number of independent liquidity providers, N_eff(rho) = N/(1 + (N-1)rho), grounded in algorithmic similarity among market makers rather than demand-side correlation or regulatory constraints. The formula collapses to 1 as rho approaches 1, capturing the idea that N nominally independent market makers provide no more diversification than a single provider when they share the same AI model. The equilibrium bid-ask spread is shown to be convex in rho, and a threshold rho** is derived above which no interior market-making equilibrium with finite spreads exists, analogous to Pagano's (1989) low-liquidity trap but driven by AI signal homogeneity. The model nests the Danielsson-Shin-Zigrand (2012) common VaR constraint mechanism as a limiting case and extends the Greenwood-Thesmar (2011) fragility concept from demand-side correlation to supply-side algorithmic correlation.

## AI and financial markets

The empirical and applied literature on AI in finance provides the motivation for the theoretical channels. Gu, Kelly, and Xiu (2020, RFS) document that diverse machine learning methods, including penalised regressions, gradient-boosted trees, and neural networks, converge on the same dominant predictors: momentum, liquidity, and volatility. This convergence is the empirical manifestation of rho approaching one. Kim, Muhn, and Nikolaev (2024) show that GPT-4 at temperature zero generates near-identical financial statement analyses, directly documenting belief homogenisation from large language models.

Calvano, Calzolari, Denicolo, and Pastorello (2020, AER) demonstrated in an industrial organisation context that Q-learning algorithms autonomously learn to sustain supra-competitive prices without explicit communication, establishing that AI agents can coordinate without being designed to do so. In financial markets, Dou, Goldstein, and Ji (2025a, NBER WP) show that RL-powered trading algorithms sustain collusive supra-competitive profits through strategy homogenisation and self-confirming equilibria, undermining competition, efficiency, and liquidity. Their companion paper, Dou, Goldstein, and Ji (2025b, SSRN), extends this to agentic AI with planning capability, showing that lookahead agents create and exploit negative bubbles through intertemporal collusion.

Danielsson, Macrae, and Uthemann (2022, JBF) provide the most comprehensive qualitative analysis of AI and systemic risk, arguing that AI homogenises risk perceptions across financial institutions, that similar perceptions lead to similar actions, and that the result is lower volatility but fatter tails and greater procyclicality. They identify four channels: information processing, common data, speed, and strategic complementarities. However, the paper is entirely narrative and proposes no formal model.

The distinction between the RL/behavioural approach and the information-theoretic approach is important. The Dou-Goldstein-Ji programme and Yang (2024) model AI fragility as arising from emergent coordination through repeated strategic interaction or planning capability. The mechanism in this paper is fundamentally different: fragility arises from signal homogeneity driven by common data and model architecture, captured by the cross-sectional correlation parameter rho. The two approaches address different sources of AI-driven fragility and are complementary rather than competing.

## Cross-channel interaction and systemic risk

The most developed multi-channel framework in the AI and finance literature is the Danielsson-Uthemann research programme (2022, JBF; 2025, JFS). The 2022 paper identifies four qualitative channels through which AI amplifies financial vulnerability. The 2025 paper formalises one of these channels (coordination/speed) using a global games model with the mu parameterisation. The other channels remain qualitative. No fixed-point characterisation of the joint system is attempted, and no compound fragility threshold is derived.

Goldstein, Huang, and Yang (2025, ARFE) provide a comprehensive survey of the theoretical forces generating market fragility, covering learning from prices, strategic complementarities, and amplification mechanisms. Their framework is general and does not address AI-specific mechanisms or the signal correlation parameterisation.

The network-based systemic risk literature, including Acemoglu, Ozdaglar, and Tahbaz-Salehi (2015) and related work, studies propagation through financial networks via balance sheet linkages and counterparty exposure. These models address a different source of systemic risk (network topology) than the signal correlation mechanism studied here. The robust-yet-fragile property identified in network models, where dense interconnections are stabilising up to a threshold and destabilising beyond it, is conceptually related to the non-monotonicity in rho but operates through a different formal mechanism.

No existing paper characterises the joint dynamics of coordination failure, information acquisition collapse, and correlated liquidity withdrawal through a common parameterisation of signal homogeneity. The amplification loop identified in this paper, with its fixed-point in (rho_eff, theta*, N_eff) and the bifurcation result that the compound fragility threshold lies strictly below any individual channel threshold, has no precedent in the literature. The application of Brouwer's fixed-point theorem to a multi-channel financial fragility system on a compact convex subset of R^3 is novel. The economic insight that moderate signal correlation, appearing safe when assessed against any single risk channel, can be systemically dangerous when all three channels interact, has been expressed qualitatively by Danielsson, Macrae, and Uthemann (2022) but never formalised.

## Summary of contributions relative to the literature

Five contributions emerge from the analysis of the literature gaps. First, the analytical characterisation of the crisis threshold theta*(rho) in the Goldstein-Pauzner bank-run framework extends the global games literature by parameterising signal correlation through AI model homogeneity, filling the gap between Hellwig's (2002) theoretical result and the AI applications of Yang (2024) and Danielsson and Uthemann (2025), neither of which derives the threshold as a function of signal correlation or characterises the uniqueness/multiplicity boundary.

Second, the cross-sectional Grossman-Stiglitz extension with two information types differentiated by correlation fills the gap in the information acquisition literature where Dugast and Foucault (2018, 2025) model temporal tradeoffs but not cross-sectional signal correlation, and Farboodi and Veldkamp (2020) model technology growth but not the correlation structure of AI outputs. The non-monotonicity of revelatory price efficiency in rho, where AI improves forecasting efficiency at low correlation but destroys informational diversity at high correlation, has no predecessor.

Third, the N_eff(rho) liquidity fragility index extends the market microstructure literature by formalising how algorithmic similarity among market makers collapses the effective number of independent liquidity providers. This extends the demand-side fragility measure of Greenwood and Thesmar (2011) and the regulatory-constraint mechanism of Danielsson, Shin, and Zigrand (2012) to the AI context, with a no-equilibrium threshold that has not been derived in any existing paper.

Fourth, and most importantly, the fixed-point amplification loop connecting all three channels through the composite operator on (rho_eff, theta*, N_eff) fills the central gap in the literature: the absence of a formal model characterising how AI-driven fragility mechanisms interact and amplify. The bifurcation result, showing that the joint threshold is strictly below the minimum of the individual thresholds, formalises the qualitative insight of Danielsson, Macrae, and Uthemann (2022) and provides the theoretical foundation for a "safety illusion" where channel-by-channel risk assessment understates systemic vulnerability.

Fifth, the endogenous rho extension, modelling competitive AI adoption as a prisoner's dilemma that drives signal correlation above the fragility threshold, connects to the emerging literature on algorithmic monoculture (Kleinberg and Raghavan, 2021; Peng, Kleinberg, Raghavan, and Lipton, 2024) and AI adoption externalities (Gans, 2023) but applies these ideas to financial markets with explicit fragility channels. The diversity mandate that bounds rho below the fragility threshold provides a formal basis for macroprudential AI regulation that the existing policy literature has discussed only informally.
